Summary

MacArthur preaches a dispensationalist exposition of Revelation 21:1–3, arguing that the present universe will be "unccreated" and replaced by a literal new heaven and new earth containing the New Jerusalem as its capital. The sermon's pastoral thesis is that Christians are insufficiently "heavenly-minded" and that the comfort of Western culture has made the church worldly. Along the way MacArthur dismisses creation-care ("save the planet people are really wasting their time"), reads Ecclesiastes 7:1 as "better to die than to be born," characterizes the world's cities as uniformly unholy, offers a 5-minute astronomy illustration with several factually wrong distance figures, and takes a brief swipe at evolution. Unlike much of MacArthur's public corpus, this particular sermon is largely free of politically charged rhetoric — no identifiable anti-Muslim, anti-LGBTQ, misogynistic, anti-Jewish, or Christian-nationalist content is present. The audit's concerns are primarily Axis 1 (tension between heaven-escape framing and Jesus's this-world kingdom teaching, plus notable absence of Matt 25 and the Lord's Prayer) and Axis 3 (multiple astronomy figures off by orders of magnitude, plus an overstated "blood is 90% water" claim).

Earth as 'disposable' — the escape strand preached as the whole canon

Topic: What God intends to do with the material creation
Why flagged: The pastor presents one canonical strand (earth-replacement) as the Bible's single voice while omitting the renewal strand (Rom 8; Col 1:20; Isa 65 read whole) and underreading his own preaching text (Rev 21:3 God-comes-down). The ethical conclusion drawn — that creation care is 'wasting their time' — is derived from the selective reading.

Canonical analysis

The canon holds two images of creation's end in unresolved tension: destruction/replacement (2 Pet 3:10–13; aspects of Rev 20–21) and renewal/reconciliation (Rom 8:19–23; Col 1:20; Isa 65 read whole; Rev 21:3 itself). The Greek of 2 Pet 3:10 is textually contested — the oldest manuscripts read 'will be found' (heurethēsetai), not 'will be burned up.' The sermon's own preaching text (Rev 21:3) envisions God coming DOWN to dwell with humanity on a renewed earth — an incarnational direction, not an evacuation. Isaiah 65:17 (cited by MacArthur) keeps going: vs. 21–22 describe people building houses and planting vineyards — continuity, not just replacement. Rev 11:18 promises judgment on 'the destroyers of the earth' — a verse that makes little sense if the earth is meant to be destroyed anyway. MacArthur preaches the 'disposable' strand as if it were the canon's single voice and draws the ethical conclusion (creation care is pointless) from it. Even within a conservative evangelical hermeneutic (N.T. Wright, Richard Bauckham, Douglas Moo, Jonathan Moo) this is a minority reading. The practical upshot — 'I want to take that burden off your back' — discourages the congregation from engaging a canonical vocation on the grounds of an uncertain eschatological mechanism.

New Jerusalem read only as destination, not as pattern for the church's life now

Topic: How Rev 21 should shape present Christian practice
Why flagged: The pastor treats the New Jerusalem as destination only, not as pattern. Rev 21:24–26 and 22:2 (nations walking by its light, leaves for healing of the nations) are within the preaching text's immediate neighborhood but are not engaged. Canonical analysis

Rev 21–22 is a picture of the New Jerusalem drawing the nations into itself and producing healing for them. The dominant patristic, Augustinian, and much Protestant reading treats this city as both telos and pattern — a vision that forms the church's present common life and outward posture toward 'the nations' and 'the kings of the earth.' Paul's Eph 2 applies 'city / holy temple' language to the church's present existence. The sermon treats the New Jerusalem strictly as a future destination — a geographic endpoint 1,500 miles cubed that descends to the post-destruction universe. This is defensible as a dispensationalist reading, but it leaves the sermon with no practical payoff: the vision of Rev 21:3 ('God dwells with them') has no bearing on how this congregation should live differently on Monday, because the vision is reserved entirely for the post-destruction future. The wider canon reads Rev 21 and Matt 5:14 together: the city-that-comes-down shapes the city-on-a-hill. The sermon reads only the first.

“Alpha Centauri is '20 billion years away'; the North Star is '400 billion'; Betelgeuse is '880 quadrillion miles away…”

Pastor's claim (full)

Alpha Centauri is '20 billion years away'; the North Star is '400 billion'; Betelgeuse is '880 quadrillion miles away' with 'a 200 million mile diameter.'

Evidence the pastor cited

None. Delivered as precise astronomical fact in an illustration about heaven's distance.

What the evidence shows

All three figures are significantly wrong relative to modern astronomical consensus. (1) Alpha Centauri: ~4.37 light-years, which is ~25.7 trillion miles; '20 billion years' is a category error (years, not miles) and even reading it charitably as '20 billion miles' is off by ~1,300×. (2) Polaris (North Star): ~433 light-years ≈ ~2.5 quadrillion miles, not 400 billion; off by roughly 6,000×. (3) Betelgeuse: distance ~500–550 light-years ≈ ~3.1 quadrillion miles, not 880 quadrillion — off by ~280×; and Betelgeuse's diameter is estimated at roughly 700 million miles (~1.2 billion km), not 200 million. The figures are presented with the rhetorical confidence of a fact-check-ready illustration; they do not survive one. The underlying theological point (the universe is vast) is unaffected, but delivering wrong figures with authority undermines pulpit credibility on verifiable claims.

What the evidence shows

The mean Earth–Moon distance is approximately 238,855 miles; perigee ~225,000 mi and apogee ~252,000 mi. 211,463 miles falls below even the closest recorded perigee and does not correspond to any standard NASA reference figure. The walking-time arithmetic is also internally inconsistent with the figure: 211,463 ÷ 24 ≈ 8,811 days ≈ 24.1 years, not 27. Minor error, low stakes, but delivered with specificity.

“'Your blood is 90% water; your flesh is 65% water.'”

Pastor's claim (full)

'Your blood is 90% water; your flesh is 65% water.'

Evidence the pastor cited

None.

What the evidence shows

Human blood plasma is approximately 92% water, but plasma makes up only ~55% of whole blood; the remainder (red and white cells, platelets) is cellular mass. Whole-blood water content is therefore closer to 50%, not 90%. The 90% figure applies to plasma specifically. 'Flesh is 65% water' is roughly defensible for overall adult body composition (~60% in men, ~55% in women) and skeletal-muscle tissue is ~75% water — the 65% figure sits inside the plausible range. The substantive point (humans and earth are water-dependent) is correct; the specific blood figure materially overstates whole-blood water content.
