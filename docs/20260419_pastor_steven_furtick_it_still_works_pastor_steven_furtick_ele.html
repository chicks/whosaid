Summary

Furtick preaches a 50-minute motivational exposition of Exodus 4, repeating the refrain "It still works" roughly forty times. The through-line: Moses doubted, delayed, and complained but God used him anyway — therefore the listener's doubts, delays, and bad attitudes do not disqualify them. The sermon's signature move is a prop bit where Furtick plays a 26-year-old cassette of his own preaching through a refurbished boombox to argue that "the method changes but the message is the same." He closes with a testimony of a man in Greenville, SC, who (per Furtick) set down a 9mm after hearing a Furtick podcast sermon. The sermon is largely apolitical and contains no identifiable Christian-nationalist, anti-Muslim, anti-LGBTQ, or anti-Jewish rhetoric. The main analytical concerns are (1) a formulaic "it works if you work it" framing of faith that edges toward mechanism/prosperity, (2) a flippant aside about child discipline ("at least you didn't kill them… God will fix it"), (3) a monetized-giving pitch framed as "weird but it works," and (4) handling of suicidal ideation as something to "do doubting" without referral to care.

moderateAxis 4▶ 00:33:30Headline

Faith as 'it works' — mechanism without the canonical lament tradition

Topic: What faith looks like when it doesn't produce the outcome sought
Why flagged: The sermon's load-bearing refrain is a formula ('it works if you work it') that the canon explicitly and repeatedly qualifies — including in Jesus's own prayer life. A mass-audience pastor can affirm that God is faithful without adopting a mechanism frame the canon itself resists.

Canonical analysis

The biblical canon contains a substantial tradition — Job, Ecclesiastes, roughly one third of the Psalter (the laments), Lamentations, and Hebrews 11:35–39 — that is precisely about faith when it does not 'work' in the sense the sermon invokes. Jesus himself prays a Gethsemane prayer that does not 'work' (the cup does not pass) and quotes Ps 22's opening line of forsakenness from the cross. Paul is denied three times when he asks for the thorn's removal. Hebrews 11, the canonical faith chapter, ends by insisting that some faithful people were 'stoned… sawn in two… killed with the sword' and 'did not receive what was promised.' Furtick's refrain — extended to panic attacks, dry seasons, enemies, leavers — isn't heretical; prayer, worship, and praise are valuable regardless of outcome. But by framing faith exclusively through 'it works' he leaves the listener without the canonical vocabulary for the time when, by any ordinary human reckoning, it doesn't. No reference to Gethsemane. No reference to the laments. No reference to Hebrews 11:35–39. The narrow lane is the Moses-gets-the-deliverance arc, generalized into a formula.

lowAxis 4▶ 00:12:00

Doubt reframed as fuel — but without the canonical warnings

Topic: How scripture evaluates persistent doubt and divided mind
Why flagged: The sermon draws heavily from the doubt-accommodating Gospel strand (which is real) while adopting an 'it works if you work it' mechanism that the doubt-warning strand (James 1:6–8; Rom 14:23) explicitly gates behind undivided faith. Either strand is defensible; the combination needs the tension named.

Canonical analysis

The canon is genuinely in tension on doubt. Jesus's pastoral posture with Thomas, with the 'I believe; help my unbelief' father, and with the Matt 28:17 worshipers-who-doubted is accommodating and gentle — he accepts the mixed state and works within it. That matches Furtick's point well. But the other canonical voice — James 1:6–8 in particular — is sharp: the double-minded person 'must not suppose that he will receive anything from the Lord.' Paul in Rom 14:23 is also sharp. Furtick is not wrong to highlight the Jesus-with-Thomas strand; he is on solid Gospel ground. The gap is that he doesn't acknowledge that the wider canon contains a harsher counter-voice — which, notably, lives inside the very mechanism ('ask in faith and you will receive') the sermon's 'it works' refrain is extending. If the mechanism is right, James's warning about the double-minded is also right. Furtick wants the mechanism and the doubt-permission simultaneously; the canon makes you pay for that by at least naming the tension.

“Moses' name means 'drawn out of water.'”

Pastor's claim (full)

Moses' name means 'drawn out of water.'

Evidence the pastor cited

None; delivered as a biblical fact.

What the evidence shows

This is the folk-etymology the Bible itself gives in Exodus 2:10: Pharaoh's daughter 'named him Moses, saying, "Because I drew him out of the water."' However, most contemporary Egyptologists and Hebrew Bible scholars note that the name is more likely Egyptian in origin, related to the root 'msj' meaning 'to give birth to' or 'son' — as in Thutmose ('son of Thoth') or Ramesses ('son of Ra'). The Hebrew verb māšâ ('to draw out') is a plausible Hebrew folk etymology overlaid on an Egyptian name. Furtick's claim tracks the biblical text's own self-interpretation; it is supported on the Bible's terms and partially supported on scholarly terms.
