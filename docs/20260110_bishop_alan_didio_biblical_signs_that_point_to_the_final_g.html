Topic: Whether Christians can read calendars and signs to identify the timing of Christ's return
Why flagged: The sermon's central rhetorical claim — 'you better figure out what time it is' — is the exact thing Jesus said is not for us to know. The pastor uses a verse (2 Pet 3:8) given to suppress this kind of reckoning to do this kind of reckoning.

Canonical analysis

The sermon does the precise thing 2 Pet 3:8 was given to *prevent*. Peter's 'a day is with the Lord as a thousand years' is given as a reason for *patience and humility* about timing — God's reckoning is not ours. The sermon flips it into a numeric formula that converts the seven days of Genesis into a 7,000-year timeline, places us at year 5,950, and pinpoints March 2026 as the inauguration of the final jubilee. This is a 19th–20th century interpretive innovation (Clarence Larkin, named in the sermon, popularized the chart in 1918) layered onto a fringe reading of Essene chronology. The dominant canonical witness — Jesus, Paul, Peter, Daniel, Deuteronomy — runs the opposite direction: the timing is hidden, the day is thief-like, the question is not for us to ask. Notably, every previous date-setting in evangelical history (1844 Miller; 1988 Whisenant; 2011 Camping; 2017 Meade) has produced confident chronologies and zero correct returns. Pastoral history alone counsels against the genre.

Why flagged: The sermon ends pitching a prosperity-confession book; the canonical witness on wealth (Jesus, James, Paul, Acts, Proverbs) is overwhelmingly cautionary. Canonical analysis

The canonical witness on wealth is overwhelmingly cautionary. Jesus issues a *woe* to the rich (Luke 6:24), tells the rich young ruler to sell everything (Mark 10), warns that you cannot serve God and money (Matt 6:24), and grounds his own mission in Isaiah 58 — economic liberation of the poor, not 'wealth transfer' to the faithful. James pronounces judgment on the luxurious. 1 Timothy says the desire to be rich plunges people into ruin. The early church in Acts 2 redistributes possessions; it does not accumulate them. Proverbs 30 explicitly asks for neither poverty nor riches. A book teaching 'how to align your mouth with heaven's economy and unlock the blessings of God' through 'prosperity confessions' is sourced from a small handful of OT verses (Deut 8:18, Mal 3:10) and an idiosyncratic reading of 3 John 2 — and is silent on the dominant canonical voice. The mid-sermon attack on the *American Gospel* documentary works to inoculate hearers against this canonical witness before it is heard.

What the evidence shows

Several distinct claims are bundled here. (1) The Essenes did use a solar 364-day calendar attested in the Dead Sea Scrolls (e.g., 4Q317–330, the Mishmarot texts) — that part is mainstream. (2) The Essenes did expect two messianic figures (the Messiah of Aaron and the Messiah of Israel), and 11QMelchizedek does feature a Melchizedek figure. (3) However, the specific claim that the Essenes predicted a Messiah dying for sins in 32 AD is not supported by the Dead Sea Scrolls scholarly consensus (Vermes, Collins, VanderKam, Charlesworth). The scrolls expect messianic figures but do not pinpoint AD 32 nor describe a substitutionary atonement. (4) The '5,950 / 7-day chart' framework is from Clarence Larkin's *Dispensational Truth* (1918) and Ussher-style biblical chronology, not from the Essenes. Larkin's chart is not Essene material. (5) The notion that Paul, John the Baptist, Hippolytus and Irenaeus all draw on a unified Essene chronology is not supported in the patristic or NT scholarly literature. The framing collapses several distinct sources into a single confident chronology. A careful claim might be 'some Second Temple Jewish texts, including some at Qumran, divided history into thousand-year ages.' The claim as delivered overstates substantially.
