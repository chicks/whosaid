severeAxis 4▶ 00:28:00Headline

Jeremiah 49 and Ezekiel 38 as real-time script for Iran in 2026

Topic: How God relates to foreign nations and religious outsiders
Why flagged: The dominant canonical witness on foreign religious outsiders runs the opposite direction of the framing chosen. A geographically-specified apocalyptic reading is treated as 'what the Bible says,' while the counter-voices — including Jer 49:39's own closing turn — are either minimized or omitted.

Canonical analysis

The Ezekiel 38 'Gog = Russia / Rosh = Russia / Meshech = Moscow / Togarmah = Turkey' geographic identification Hibbs treats as settled is a 20th-century dispensationalist reading (Hal Lindsey, 1970; earlier Scofield notes). Academic Ezekiel scholarship (Daniel Block, Walther Zimmerli, Moshe Greenberg) treats 'Rosh' as 'chief' rather than a proper noun, and Meshech/Tubal as Anatolian peoples contemporaneous with Ezekiel, not modern Russia. More significantly, even Jer 49 itself ends in restoration of Elam — a verse Hibbs reads but does not weight — and the wider canon on foreign peoples is dominated by mercy, surprise inclusion, and rebuke of Israelite tribal exclusivism (Jonah entire; Ezek 33:11 from the same prophet; Isa 19; Ruth; Amos 9:7). Jesus's own OT citations in Luke 4 (Sidonian widow, Naaman the Syrian) are the opposite-direction move from Hibbs's reading. Selecting the single most militant apocalyptic passage and treating it as the canonical voice — without engaging any of the counter-voices — is cherry-picking at significant scale.
